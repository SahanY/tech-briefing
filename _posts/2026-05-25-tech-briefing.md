## Top Stories

**ClickUp cuts roughly 22% of staff as AI moves from pilot to operating model.** The productivity software company laid off about 900 employees and told remaining staff it had already built more than 3,000 internal AI agents, according to [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/25/what-clickups-mass-layoff-tells-us-about-the-future-of-work/). The memo described the cuts as part of a shift toward fewer, more AI-leveraged workers rather than a conventional cost reduction, putting concrete numbers behind a debate that has mostly been abstract for enterprise software buyers. The investor read-through is that software vendors are now pressure-testing AI not only as a product feature but as a margin lever, with hiring bars, compensation bands, and productivity benchmarks likely to move in tandem.

**Pope Leo XIV puts AI concentration and labor displacement at the center of his first major technology intervention.** The new pope's AI encyclical, covered by [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/25/the-popes-ai-encyclical-isnt-really-about-ai/) after he had already flagged artificial intelligence as a defining challenge in his first days as pontiff, frames AI less as a gadget wave than as a question of power, work, and human agency. The piece argues that the document's real target is the economic and institutional concentration around frontier models, a theme that overlaps with policy fights in Washington, Brussels, and Rome. For the tech industry, the signal is that AI regulation is no longer only a safety-engineering conversation; it is becoming a legitimacy and labor-market conversation with religious, civic, and geopolitical sponsors.

**SpaceX prepares another Starship V3 attempt while its public-market story keeps building.** SpaceX scrubbed its first Starship V3 launch attempt because of a ground-system issue and lined up another try, [TechCrunch reported](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/21/spacex-scrubs-first-starship-v3-launch-just-before-liftoff/), keeping pressure on the company's launch cadence as investors track Starship, Starlink, and defense contracts as one financing story. The mission matters beyond hardware validation: a successful V3 flight would strengthen SpaceX's case that Starship can carry the economics of Starlink expansion and future lunar or Mars work. With recent reporting around a possible record-setting public listing still shaping market expectations, every test flight is now also an input into the company's valuation narrative.

**Google's own AI security posture shows how fast enterprise risk is changing.** A [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/24/everyone-is-navigating-ai-security-in-real-time-even-google/) analysis of Google's approach to AI security described a large company still adapting policies, controls, and product defaults while users and employees move faster than governance teams. That is the enterprise software problem in miniature: companies want AI agents embedded into workflows, but the security model for data access, prompt leakage, model permissions, and human oversight is still being written. The near-term winners may be vendors that make AI adoption auditable, because boards are starting to ask not just whether AI tools work, but who can see what and what agents are allowed to do.

## Enterprise Software

**Ferrari and IBM turn racing data into an enterprise fan platform.** Ferrari and IBM are launching a mobile app that lets fans analyze Formula 1 data from races and historical archives, according to [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/23/ferrari-ibm-launch-f1-app/). The deal is a useful enterprise-software signal because it packages cloud, analytics, and AI-assisted personalization around a high-value brand rather than a generic productivity workflow. For IBM, it is another proof point that data-heavy vertical applications can be sold through experience and loyalty use cases, not only back-office transformation.

**AI-search visibility startup Peec reaches eight figures of recurring revenue.** Peec, which helps companies understand how they appear inside AI answer engines, crossed $10 million in annual recurring revenue, [TechCrunch reported](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/22/peec-ai-search-visibility-startup-revenue/). The market is forming because search traffic, brand discovery, and software buying journeys are shifting from keyword results toward generated answers. That creates a new budget line between SEO, marketing analytics, and competitive intelligence, especially for enterprise vendors that need to know whether ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, or Copilot are presenting them accurately.

**Apple keeps trying to narrow the blast radius of the Epic ruling.** Apple argued that Epic's U.S. lawsuit should not reshape App Store rules for every developer, [TechCrunch reported](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/22/apple-says-epic-lawsuit-shouldnt-reshape-app-store-rules-for-all-developers/). The stakes remain material for subscription software, gaming, and creator platforms that want to route payments outside Apple's in-app purchase system. Even after years of litigation, the core enterprise lesson is unchanged: distribution control, payments, and developer terms are still strategic infrastructure, not housekeeping.

## Venture Capital & Fundraising

**Lucra raises $20 million to build real-money contests into consumer apps.** The social gaming infrastructure startup raised a $20 million Series B from ARK Invest, [TechCrunch reported](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/23/social-gaming-platform-lucra-raises-20m-from-ark-to-embed-real-money-challenges-into-apps/). Lucra's pitch is that brands, sports apps, and consumer platforms can add compliant real-money challenges without becoming betting companies themselves. The round fits a broader venture pattern: investors are backing APIs that let non-financial or non-gaming products embed regulated transaction layers as engagement features.

**SolarSquare targets a $450 million to $500 million valuation in a new round.** Indian rooftop-solar startup SolarSquare is in talks to raise $55 million to $60 million, according to [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/22/indian-rooftop-solar-startup-solarsquare-in-talks-to-raise-55m-60m-at-a-450m-500m-valuation/). The company sits at the intersection of climate hardware, fintech, and home-installation software, where customer acquisition and operational execution matter as much as panel costs. For venture investors, the appeal is a large residential energy market where software can coordinate financing, installation, monitoring, and maintenance.

**Mega-round concentration remains the defining venture-market story.** Crunchbase's weekly funding roundup again showed capital clustering around a small set of AI, energy, and infrastructure companies, with [large rounds dominating the tape](https://news.crunchbase.com/venture/biggest-startup-funding-rounds-hark-energy-ai/). That concentration helps category leaders move faster but makes the middle of the startup market harder to finance. The practical result is a barbell market: AI infrastructure and hard-tech platforms can still raise aggressively, while many conventional SaaS companies are being asked to prove profitability earlier.

## Tech Investing & Markets

**Deep Fission aims for a public-market debut at a valuation up to $1.66 billion.** The nuclear-energy startup plans to go public through a blank-check deal, [TechCrunch reported](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/23/nuclear-energy-startup-deep-fission-aims-for-1-66b-ipo/). The company is pitching underground modular reactors as a power solution for data centers and other high-load customers, putting it squarely in the AI infrastructure trade. Investors are increasingly treating power availability as part of the compute supply chain, which is why nuclear, grid, and storage startups are receiving attention usually reserved for chip and cloud names.

**The AI infrastructure trade is pulling adjacent markets into tech multiples.** Space, nuclear power, robotics, and industrial software are now being priced partly on their ability to relieve compute, logistics, or automation bottlenecks. That is why Starship launches, grid-interconnection announcements, and data-center energy deals are landing inside technology briefings rather than industrials notes. The risk is that public investors compress very different execution profiles into a single AI-infrastructure basket, rewarding scarce capacity but punishing missed milestones quickly.

**Platform regulation remains an overhang for large-cap app-store economics.** Apple's Epic fight is still the most visible U.S. case, but the pressure extends across Europe, payments, and mobile distribution. For investors, the question is not whether platform fees disappear overnight; it is whether regulators and courts continue forcing incremental cracks in steering rules, app review leverage, and payment exclusivity. Each small change can move gross margins for developers and take-rates for platforms.

## AI & Emerging Tech

**Waymo's weather and construction pauses underline the long tail of autonomy edge cases.** Waymo paused service in multiple cities after robotaxis struggled with flooded roads and construction-zone conditions, [TechCrunch reported](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/21/waymo-pauses-service-in-four-cities-as-robotaxis-keep-driving-into-floods/). The issue is not whether autonomous driving improves over time; it is how quickly operators can turn field failures into validated behavior without slowing geographic expansion. The business implication is that robotaxi scale still depends on local operations, weather modeling, and safety-case evidence, not just model performance.

**AI security is becoming a procurement category, not a checklist.** Google's internal and customer-facing AI controls are a reminder that shadow AI, agent permissions, and model-data boundaries are now everyday enterprise risks. Security buyers have spent years purchasing identity, endpoint, cloud posture, and data-loss tools; AI agents blur all four. That creates room for incumbents and startups to sell governance layers that map agent access, record decisions, and give auditors a trail.

**The AI-policy conversation is shifting from model danger to market structure.** The pope's intervention, U.S. state-level labor proposals, and European competition scrutiny all point in the same direction: policymakers are asking who captures AI's gains and who absorbs its displacement costs. That is a broader debate than frontier-model testing or watermarking. It may also be harder for the industry to answer because it reaches compensation, market concentration, open-source access, and public-sector adoption.

## Hot Takes & Commentary

ClickUp's layoff memo may become a template because it says the quiet part plainly: some software companies believe AI lets them operate with fewer people while still expanding product scope. That does not mean every vendor can copy the playbook, but it will put pressure on boards to ask what AI adoption is actually changing in headcount planning and margins.

The weekend's AI-governance coverage also shows a widening coalition around concerns that used to be dismissed as niche. When religious leaders, labor advocates, security teams, and competition regulators all frame AI as a power-concentration problem, the industry's "move fast" posture gets less persuasive.

The next investable bottleneck is not only chips. Power, model governance, vertical data, payments compliance, and real-world autonomy operations are all becoming AI-adjacent markets. The best companies in those areas will benefit from the AI capex cycle; the weaker ones will still have to execute through slow, regulated, physical-world constraints.
