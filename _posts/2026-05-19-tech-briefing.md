## Top Stories

**Google and Blackstone are turning AI compute into an infrastructure product.** Blackstone announced a joint venture with Google to build a new U.S.-based TPU cloud company, with Blackstone committing an initial $5 billion in equity and the first 500 megawatts of capacity targeted for 2027. The company will package data center capacity, operations, networking, and access to Google Cloud Tensor Processing Units as a compute-as-a-service offering, giving customers another route to Google silicon outside the standard Google Cloud buying motion. ([Blackstone](https://www.blackstone.com/news/press/blackstone-announces-joint-venture-with-google-to-create-new-tpu-cloud/), [Reuters via Investing.com](https://www.investing.com/news/stock-market-news/google-blackstone-to-create-new-ai-cloud-company-wsj-reports-4697320))

The move matters because AI infrastructure is increasingly a financing problem, not only a chip problem. Blackstone brings power, real estate, project finance, and infrastructure balance-sheet capacity; Google brings TPUs, model demand, and cloud software. The venture also gives enterprises and AI labs another credible alternative to Nvidia-centric GPU capacity, while reinforcing the idea that AI compute will be funded like energy, logistics, and telecom infrastructure.

**Google I/O centered on agentic AI.** Google used I/O to roll out Gemini 3.5 Flash and preview a broader push around Gemini Spark, Search agents, developer tooling, and enterprise workflows. AP described agentic AI as a central focus of the conference, while CIO Dive reported that Gemini 3.5 Flash is positioned as a lighter-weight model for agentic work and that Gemini Spark is designed to reason across connected apps. ([AP](https://apnews.com/article/a984e6756032dc4af260f8fa27e8f4a9), [CIO Dive](https://www.ciodive.com/news/google-unveils-Gemini-agentic-models/820783/), [Android Central](https://www.androidcentral.com/apps-software/google-thinks-gemini-3-5-flash-can-finally-make-ai-agents-more-useful))

The enterprise implication is that Google is trying to collapse the distance between model demos and work execution. If Gemini can act across Search, Workspace, Android, developer environments, and connected enterprise tools, Google has a distribution advantage few AI labs can match. The risk is complexity: agentic products need reliability, permissions, auditability, and cost controls before CIOs can deploy them broadly.

**OpenAI defeated Elon Musk in a high-profile trial.** A federal jury unanimously found that Musk waited too long to sue OpenAI, Sam Altman, Greg Brockman, and Microsoft, ending the remaining claims on statute-of-limitations grounds. Axios framed the outcome as a major defeat for Musk and a removal of liability risk for OpenAI and Microsoft; Le Monde reported Musk planned to appeal and objected to the ruling as procedural. ([Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/05/18/musk-loses-ai-trial-openai-altman), [Axios analysis](https://www.axios.com/2026/05/19/musk-altman-openai-trial), [Le Monde](https://www.lemonde.fr/en/economy/article/2026/05/18/musk-s-lawsuit-against-openai-dismissed-due-to-statute-of-limitations_6753577_19.html))

The verdict does not settle every public question about OpenAI's shift from nonprofit origins to a capital-intensive AI company, but it removes a large legal distraction as OpenAI prepares for a potential public-market path. It also keeps attention on the larger governance question: frontier AI companies are now infrastructure-scale businesses that need continuous financing, and the original nonprofit language around AI safety has collided with the economics of data centers, chips, and distribution.

## Enterprise Software

**Dell pushed deeper into AI-ready enterprise infrastructure.** At Dell Technologies World, Dell introduced PowerStore Elite, a refreshed storage platform the company says can deliver up to three times more performance than prior models and reduce manual array-management work through built-in AI. The launch fits Dell's broader AI Factory positioning: enterprises want faster infrastructure, but they also want simpler operations as AI workloads make capacity planning less predictable. ([ITPro](https://www.itpro.com/infrastructure/servers-and-storage/dell-powerstore-elite-unveiled-at-dell-technologies-world-2026))

Dell also highlighted AI Factory updates with Nvidia, while Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang told Dell's conference audience that the industry had entered the era of "useful AI." That message is aimed squarely at enterprise buyers: the pitch is no longer only about training frontier models, but about deploying private, governed, production AI systems in existing corporate environments. ([TechRadar](https://www.techradar.com/pro/ive-got-big-ambitions-now-nvidia-ceo-jensen-huang-says-were-finally-in-the-era-of-useful-ai-and-things-will-only-get-faster), [Dell](https://www.dell.com/en-us/dt/corporate/newsroom/announcements/detailpage.press-releases~usa~2026~05~mistral-ai-powers-ai-innovation-on-dell-technologies-infrastructure.htm))

**Google's agent platform story is becoming an enterprise software story.** Gemini 3.5 Flash and Spark were consumer-visible announcements, but the deeper enterprise angle is workflow automation. CIO Dive noted that Google is competing with other AI providers for enterprise customers deploying automation, with tools that can reason across connected apps and internal workflows. The obvious upside is stickier Workspace and Cloud usage; the hard part is convincing regulated customers that agentic systems can be supervised and governed like traditional software.

## Venture Capital & Fundraising

**Decart raised $300 million as AI infrastructure investors chase chip flexibility.** Axios reported that Decart, a real-time generative AI research lab, raised $300 million at nearly a $4 billion valuation, led by Radical Ventures with participation from Benchmark, Sequoia, Zeev Ventures, and Aleph VC. Other coverage described Decart's focus as real-time world models and hardware-agnostic optimization software that can help models move across Nvidia GPUs, AWS Trainium, and Google TPUs. ([Axios Pro Rata](https://www.axios.com/newsletters/axios-pro-rata-d969e2c3-8307-4952-be0c-ea0c8e3fb2f8), [The Next Web](https://thenextweb.com/news/decart-300-million-radical-ventures-world-models))

The round sits directly inside the AI-infrastructure bottleneck. If model builders can run workloads efficiently across multiple chip families, the market becomes less dependent on one vendor's supply, software stack, and pricing. That is why a company like Decart can be both a Nvidia portfolio asset and a reference point for Amazon or Google silicon: the value is in portability and optimization.

**Cerebras kept the IPO window open for AI infrastructure.** TechCrunch reported that Cerebras raised $5.5 billion in its IPO, pricing above the marketed range and then surging in its debut. VentureBeat noted the offering gave the AI chipmaker a large war chest for buildout, while also highlighting the market's willingness to fund alternatives to Nvidia when the story is tied to AI data center capacity. ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/14/cerebras-raises-5-5b-kicking-off-2026s-ipo-season-with-a-bang/), [VentureBeat](https://venturebeat.com/technology/cerebras-stock-nearly-doubles-on-day-one-as-ai-chipmaker-hits-100-billion-what-it-means-for-ai-infrastructure))

## Tech Investing & Markets

**Nvidia earnings became the market's next AI test.** Investors were looking ahead to Nvidia's May 20 report as a bellwether for AI demand across chips, memory, networking, servers, cooling, and power. Kiplinger noted Nvidia shares were up meaningfully year to date heading into the print, while Investing.com reported that tech stocks erased earlier declines a day before the earnings event. ([Kiplinger](https://www.kiplinger.com/investing/live/nvidia-earnings-live-updates-and-commentary-may-2026), [Investing.com](https://www.investing.com/news/stock-market-news/wall-st-futures-steady-after-techled-selloff-nvidia-results-awaited-4697391))

The setup matters because the AI trade has broadened beyond Nvidia but still takes its cues from Nvidia. A strong outlook would support the idea that hyperscaler and enterprise AI demand remains supply-constrained; any disappointment would pressure the entire AI infrastructure stack, including chip alternatives, server OEMs, memory suppliers, and power/data-center names.

**SpaceX IPO watch intensified after the OpenAI verdict.** Axios Pro Rata wrote that SpaceX was expected to publicly file its S-1 within 48 hours, and that Musk losing to OpenAI removed one distraction ahead of the listing process. The potential IPO remains one of the biggest private-market events in tech, with investors watching how SpaceX packages launch, Starlink, and its AI-related ambitions for public markets. ([Axios Pro Rata](https://www.axios.com/newsletters/axios-pro-rata-d969e2c3-8307-4952-be0c-ea0c8e3fb2f8))

## AI & Emerging Tech

The day's AI news clustered around a single theme: agents need infrastructure. Google's Gemini announcements focused on multi-step work; Dell and Nvidia pushed AI factories for enterprise deployment; Decart raised capital for model portability; and Blackstone's Google venture treated compute as a long-duration asset class. The more AI systems move from chat to action, the more the bottlenecks shift to compute availability, governance, latency, storage, and enterprise integration.

Meta's restructuring added a labor-market edge to the same story. ITPro reported that an internal memo suggested Meta would cut 10% of employees and transfer another 10% as it refocused around AI, with cuts expected to begin this week. Whether or not AI is directly replacing roles, the largest platforms are clearly reorganizing cost structures around AI infrastructure spending. ([ITPro](https://www.itpro.com/business/leadership/internal-memo-suggests-meta-will-lay-off-10-percent-of-its-employees-with-a-further-10-percent-set-to-be-transferred-to-better-focus-on-ai))

## Hot Takes & Commentary

The emerging market structure is becoming clearer: AI companies are turning into infrastructure companies, and infrastructure investors are turning into AI investors. That convergence favors vendors with capital access, power strategy, and deep distribution. It also raises a strategic question for enterprise software buyers: the most important AI procurement decisions may be less about which chatbot is best and more about which platform controls the compute, data, agents, and governance layer underneath.

The Musk/OpenAI trial underscored the same shift from a different angle. The public narrative around AI may still use mission language, but the operating reality is IPO readiness, chip access, data-center financing, and enterprise distribution.
